Reflection Summary

My dad got a Canon Powershot when I was around 11 years old. It was a slim, simple, and compact digital camera
that we took everywhere and a camera that I was free to use. I mostly took photos of random things around me,
like my stuffed animals and the trees outside. Living in Charlottesville, Virginia at the time, we also had a porch with a view of the
far-off Blue Ridge Mountains. The sunsets on the porch were gorgeous, but I was always frustrated the camera never
captured how the sunset and sky really looked.

When I was in middle school, I attended a photography summer camp during the day. I learned how to shoot and develop film in the darkroom, but I was very
uninterested in shooting film. I used the digital equipment available and spent a lot of time editing and using whack effects in Photoshop. I thought the
analog film process was a bore and the end results weren’t worth the time. It’s funny that film photography has made a comeback over the past few years.
There’s a nostalgic look and feel to film lomography that isn’t available with phone cameras. I see a lot of disposable film camera apps that attempt to
mimic the feeling and look of cheap film, but nothing comes close. The return to obsolete technology and the waiting and developing photo process reflects
youth culture trend shifts towards nostalgia. It’s interesting to see it play out!

In high school, my dad got an entry-level DSLR camera (Canon Rebel) for our family, but no one used it besides me. I was excited to have access to a real
body-and-lens camera and tried it out on my friends. One of the first people I took pictures of was my friend Reagan and her boyfriend at the time, Andrew.
They were typical photos from high schoolers in Columbia - we went to the Francis Quadrangle and I got shots of them walking and holding hands by The Columns. Tacky stuff!

My senior year at Rock Bridge High, I joined yearbook. I hadn’t touched cameras for a couple years, but still used my eye and my iPhone camera.
Yearbook gave my friends and I access to professional equipment. I was able to cover events like sports games, jazz band concerts and school events,
but I was also able to use the equipment to do things like my friends’ senior photos and portrait photography. In yearbook we also had to write and interview
other students. Through this, I met a lot of people and friends. I loved how having a camera in my hand and a story to write made me a person of interest, even
though I was capturing other people. People are truer, better versions of themselves when a camera is present.

During my time at Mizzou, I didn’t really touch cameras. I wanted to, I always wrote in my journal how I wish I had reasons to take photos or people who’s stories
I could help tell. I was a business student for 2.5 years and hated my classes. The content was mostly memorization and strategy, nothing I could be creative in.
I still had an interest in hip hop, movies, and working in entertainment media and content creation. I bought a handful of disposable cameras during my summers and
developed and looked back on my memories in the fall.

In November of 2021, I reached out to Andrew, one of the first people I had photographed. Andrew has a streetwear brand and a whole skate community called Virginia Avenue
(commonly referred to as Virginia Ave). He is one of the most cultured and fashionable people I know, so it was a pleasure to be able to connect with him again.
I used my dad’s old camera and took photos in the Turner Ave parking garage on Mizzou’s campus.

I started an Instagram right after that shoot. He asked me how much for the photos and I told him I didn’t want money - I countered and asked to make and run an Instagram
account for his brand instead. It was called virginiaavemedia until recently - my username now is mybloodyvirginiaave, inspired by the Irish shoegaze band, ‘my bloody valentine’.
Since I made this account in November to showcase my photo design and members of the Virginia Ave community, I have found friends in local artists, skaters, designers,
and musicians around Mizzou and subcultures of Columbia. The feeling of community and belonging is overwhelming, and I have my camera, friends, and Instagram account
to thank for that. I’m very grateful for finding my way back to photos after feeling hopeless and out of touch with my desire to be a content creator.

I love photos because they’re so accessible - there’s no barrier to understanding and interpreting them. Social media apps have made photography one of the most
accessible mediums. Through my personal struggle to find something I love doing, my outlook on life and content creation has done a complete 180. Anything is
possible with the things you already have and the with people around you!
